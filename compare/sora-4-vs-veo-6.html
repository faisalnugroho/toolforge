When Sora 4 wins

Sora 4 is the better storyteller. When you describe a complex scene with multiple actions and a specific camera move ("a drone shot pulling back from a coffee cup as steam rises, revealing a rainy Tokyo street at dusk"), Sora 4 nails the intent more often than Veo 6. Its prompt adherence — getting the exact composition, the exact action, the exact mood you asked for — is the strongest of any video model we've tested. Physics on fast-moving objects (sports, action shots, water) is also slightly better; things collide and deform the way they should.

Camera controls (pan, tilt, zoom, dolly) are more granular and predictable. If you're doing narrative or art-direction-heavy work where you need to be the director, Sora 4 gives you more knobs.

When Veo 6 wins

Veo 6 wins on raw image quality. 4K output means you can crop, push in, and still deliver a crisp master for client work. Lip sync from a provided audio track is the best in the industry — close enough that talking-head explainers and short-form ad creative are production-usable without a re-record. Multi-character consistency (keeping the same person's face across shots) is also more reliable than Sora 4, which still drifts on faces in longer scenes.

The native audio is better at ambient sound design, and the YouTube-ready export (right aspect ratios, captions stub, correct codec) saves a render step if that's your distribution channel.

The verdict

Use Sora 4 if your priority is prompt control, camera direction, and getting the exact shot you described — narrative work, ads where you storyboard first, art-heavy pieces. Use Veo 6 if you need 4K for delivery, lip sync that doesn't look like a deepfake, and consistent characters across cuts — talking-head explainers, product shots, branded content for YouTube. Sora 4 is the better creative tool; Veo 6 is the better production tool. Agencies should have both.

Winner: Sora 4 on creative control · Veo 6 on production quality

How we tested

We generated 60 identical prompts across both models — 4K nature, talking heads, action shots, complex camera moves, multi-character scenes, product shots, abstract motion graphics. Each clip was scored blind (prompt hidden) on resolution, prompt adherence, physics, lip sync, audio quality, and consistency. We also tested image-to-video on 10 reference images.
