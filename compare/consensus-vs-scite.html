Consensus vs Scite

Both Consensus and Scite are AI search engines for academic research — but they solve different problems. Consensus pulls answers from peer-reviewed papers and tells you 'what the science says.' Scite shows you how papers cite each other, so you can see whether a citation supports, contradicts, or just mentions the claim. We tested both for 30 days across 50 real research questions.

Consensus: pros & cons

✓ Works

  • Pulls from 200M+ peer-reviewed papers
  • Clear 'consensus meter' shows strength of agreement
  • GPT-4 powered synthesis with source citations
  • Best-in-class for lit reviews and meta-analyses
  • Free tier covers most individual researchers

✗ Doesn't

  • Limited to published research (no preprints)
  • No citation context analysis
  • Free tier has query limits
  • Sometimes oversimplifies nuanced findings

Scite: pros & cons

✓ Works

  • Shows citation context (supports/contradicts)
  • Classifies how each paper cites a claim
  • Great for verifying specific scientific claims
  • Browser extension for inline fact-checking
  • Used by major publishers like Wiley and BMJ

✗ Doesn't

  • Smaller paper index than Consensus
  • No GPT-style synthesis (just citation counts)
  • Free tier very limited (5 reports/mo)
  • Citation classifications are AI-generated — verify manually
  • $20/mo is steep for casual use

The verdict

Use Consensus if you want AI-synthesized answers from the scientific literature — it's the best 'ask a research question and get a cited answer' tool in 2026. Use Scite if you need to verify specific scientific claims or see how papers cite each other. Power users should have both: Consensus for the synthesis, Scite for the verification.

How we tested

Both tools were used for 30+ days across real research workflows: literature reviews, claim verification, meta-analyses, and academic writing. We scored on paper coverage, citation accuracy, synthesis quality, ease of use, and pricing. We don't accept payment for reviews.
