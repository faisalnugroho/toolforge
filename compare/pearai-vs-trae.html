PearAI vs Trae

PearAI and Trae are both AI-native VSCode forks built in 2025 to challenge Cursor. PearAI is open source and BYOK (bring your own key). Trae is free during beta but proprietary (ByteDance-owned). We tested both for 30+ days of daily coding. Here's who wins and when.

The verdict

Use PearAI if you care about open source, want to bring your own API keys (privacy + cost control), or work at a company that won't allow proprietary AI tooling on source code. Use Trae if you want free access to Claude 3.7 and GPT-4o with zero setup and a slick AI Builder that scaffolds whole apps. PearAI is the principled choice; Trae is the convenient choice.

Winner: Trae for convenience and free top-tier models. PearAI for privacy, OSS, and cost control.

Pricing model

Trae is free during beta — no quotas, no model fees. You get Claude 3.7 and GPT-4o without paying anything. PearAI is free as a download, but you bring your own API keys and pay OpenAI/Anthropic directly. For high-volume users, PearAI can become expensive ($50-200/mo). For most devs, Trae wins on cost until pricing is announced post-beta.

Privacy and trust

PearAI wins decisively. It's Apache 2.0, code is auditable on GitHub, and your code never leaves your machine except through the model API you control. Trae is a ByteDance product — code is sent to ByteDance's servers, and the privacy policy is opt-out, not opt-in. For enterprise or regulated work, PearAI is the safer choice. For hobby projects, Trae is fine.

AI Builder feature

Trae's AI Builder is the standout feature — describe an app in natural language and get a full scaffolded codebase (Next.js + Tailwind + Prisma). PearAI has a similar feature but it's less polished and requires you to set up the underlying model. For non-technical founders or "I have an idea" workflows, Trae wins on UX.

How we tested

We coded daily for 30+ days on both IDEs — same projects, same prompts, same git workflow. We scored on inline-edit quality, multi-file Composer equivalent, language support, model variety, and privacy posture. We don't accept payment for reviews — see our full disclosure.
