AI Tools That Replaced My $4,000/Month Personal Assistant

I fired my executive assistant and rebuilt the role with AI. Here is the exact stack, what it costs, and what broke.

TF
ToolForge Editorial
✍️ Published 2026-07-17 ⏱️ 6 min read 🔄 Updated 2026-07-17

A full-time executive assistant runs $4,000+/month once you add payroll tax and benefits. In early 2026 I replaced mine with an AI stack costing under $200/month. It was not painless. Here is what actually worked, what I had to keep human, and the real numbers.

The setup that replaced her

I kept three human tasks (high-stakes relationship calls, signed checks, and anything legally binding) and routed everything else through AI: scheduling via an AI calendar, inbox triage via an AI email assistant, meeting notes via an AI notetaker, and a voice agent for inbound call screening.

What it costs now

All-in the stack runs $184/month: an AI calendar ($15), an email assistant ($30), a meeting notetaker ($18), a voice agent ($49), a writing assistant ($20), and an automation tool ($12). Plus $40 in API usage. Versus $4,000 before.

What broke (and how I fixed it)

The voice agent hallucinated a callback once. The email assistant drafted a passive-aggressive reply to a client that I caught just in time. Lesson: keep a 10-minute daily review. AI handles 90%; you own the 10% that matters.

The verdict

Would I re-hire a human? Not at this stage. The AI stack is faster, never sleeps, and scales. But it needs a human editor-in-chief. If you are paying for an assistant mostly to manage your calendar and inbox, you can replace 80% of it tomorrow.

The takeaway

Start with one task — inbox triage is the highest-leverage first win. Once that is solid, layer in scheduling, then notes, then voice. Do not boil the ocean in week one.
