Best AI Tools for DevOps Engineers in 2026

Published June 22, 2026 · 12 min read · By ToolForge Editorial

The 12 AI tools DevOps and platform engineers actually use in 2026 — for incident response, infrastructure-as-code, observability, and the endless toil. Real recommendations, real pricing.

𝕏 Share in LinkedIn Y Hacker News ✉ Email

DevOps was the first engineering discipline to be meaningfully transformed by AI, but most 'best AI tools for DevOps' lists are written by people who've never been paged at 3am. After surveying 50+ platform engineers about what they actually run, here are the 12 tools that earned a permanent spot in the stack.

The two AI use cases that actually pay off in DevOps

Most DevOps AI tools are gimmicks. The two categories that genuinely move the needle are: (1) AI-assisted incident response (compress MTTR), and (2) AI-assisted infrastructure-as-code (compress the endless toil of writing Terraform, debugging Helm charts, reviewing YAML). Everything else — AI-powered dashboards, AI log analysis with no action — is theatre.

The list below is dominated by tools that fit those two categories, plus a handful of quality-of-life tools (better terminal, better shell history, better runbook generation) that the platform engineers we surveyed universally agreed on.

One more thing: every tool here has to either (a) reduce MTTR, (b) reduce toil, or (c) make on-call less painful. Tools that don't pass one of those three tests got cut.

Incident response & on-call

This is where AI has the biggest measurable impact. The right tool can compress a 30-minute incident to 10 minutes, which over a year is worth more than the salary of an engineer.

Fireflies.ai ($19/mo) — yes, the meeting tool — also records and transcribes incident bridges. Auto-generates the post-incident review doc. Saves 30+ minutes per incident, which adds up to ~40 hours/year of engineering time.

Glean ($100+/user/mo, enterprise) connects to PagerDuty, Datadog, GitHub, Notion, and Slack. When you get paged, Glean's AI assistant pulls the relevant runbook, the last 5 similar incidents, the on-call's recent context, and the team's tribal knowledge. Cuts triage time by 50%+.

Rootly ($0-25/user/mo) is the AI-native incident management platform. Auto-creates the incident, auto-pages, auto-creates the Slack channel, auto-summarizes the resolution. Most teams that switch from PagerDuty don't go back.

Shoreline.io (custom pricing) is the AI-driven runbook automation platform. You describe a runbook in natural language, Shoreline generates the automation. Pays for itself the first time it auto-resolves an alert that would have paged someone.

Infrastructure as code & config

AI is genuinely good at generating boilerplate Terraform, Helm charts, and Kustomize overlays. It's also good at reviewing IaC PRs for security issues and anti-patterns. The tools below cover both.

Warp (free + $15/mo Pro) is the AI-powered terminal. Best-in-class command suggestions, history search, and AI agent for fixing broken commands. DevOps engineers we surveyed called it 'the first terminal upgrade in 15 years that actually matters.'

Brainboard ($19/mo) is the AI-assisted Terraform GUI. Drag-and-drop AWS/GCP/Azure infra, AI generates the Terraform, you review and merge. Cuts the time to write new infrastructure by ~70%.

Snyk ($25/dev/mo Team) is the AI-powered security scanner for IaC. Catches misconfigurations, exposed secrets, and policy violations in your Terraform/CloudFormation/Kubernetes YAML before they reach prod. The 'DeepCode AI' feature learns your team's patterns.

Firefly (custom pricing) is the AI cloud asset management platform. Auto-discovers every resource across AWS/GCP/Azure, flags drift, and suggests IaC code to bring everything under management. Solves the 'we have 400 unmanaged S3 buckets' problem.

Observability, monitoring, and debugging

AI observability tools have gotten genuinely good in 2025-2026. The leading tools can correlate logs, metrics, and traces automatically — which used to be the entire job of an SRE.

Honeycomb ($0-130/mo) is the AI-augmented observability platform. BubbleUp automatically surfaces which dimensions correlate with the error. Query Assistant lets you write complex queries in natural language.

Arize Phoenix (open source) is the AI/LLM observability platform. Tracks every prompt, response, and token cost across your LLM apps. Critical if you're running any AI in production.

Datadog (custom pricing, $$$) — the incumbent. Their AI features (Bits AI) are uneven but the Watchdog auto-detection is genuinely useful. Best if you already have Datadog; not worth migrating to if you don't.

PostHog (free up to 1M events/mo) is the AI product analytics platform. If you ship product features, this is what your PM team should be using to understand usage. The AI insights surface anomalies automatically.

Putting it together: a 12-tool DevOps stack for ~$300/mo

Total cost for the 12 tools above (assuming you're a small team, mixing free tiers and paid plans): ~$300/month. Replaces at least one full-time SRE hire, which is the largest savings category in any engineering org.

Pre-revenue or tiny teams: stick to free tiers of Warp, Snyk (limited free), Honeycomb (limited free), and PostHog (1M events free). That's a $0/month DevOps stack that covers ~60% of what you need.

Once you're past 20 engineers: add Glean and Rootly. They pay for themselves the first quarter. Once you're past 100 engineers: add Shoreline and Firefly. The ROI is dramatic but you need volume to justify the spend.
