What AI is actually useful for in therapy practice

Four categories, in order of ROI: (1) session notes — AI listens (with consent), generates SOAP/DAP/birthing notes, you edit and sign. (2) Treatment plans — AI drafts from session content, you refine. (3) Client communication — AI drafts replies to portal messages, you approve. (4) Practice admin — scheduling, billing, intake forms. AI is NOT yet useful for actual therapy itself — that's still the human clinician.

What to check before adopting (HIPAA is non-negotiable)

Five things: (1) HIPAA compliance. The tool MUST sign a BAA (Business Associate Agreement). Free tiers of ChatGPT/Claude/Notion AI do NOT — they're training on your inputs and would be a HIPAA violation. (2) Consent management. Clients must consent to AI in the session. (3) Recording disclosure. If the AI listens, disclose it. (4) Data residency. PHI should stay in US servers. (5) Audit logs. You need to be able to show what the AI saw and output.

The bottom line

If you're a solo therapist, start with Upheal or TherapyAssist for AI notes — they're HIPAA-compliant, BAA included, and pay for themselves in the first week. Add SimplePractice or Headway if you also need scheduling and billing. If you're a clinic, layer in Quenza for client engagement and ElevenLabs for psychoeducational audio. The total monthly cost (~$150-250) is less than one extra client session — and it gives you 5+ hours back every week to see more clients or actually rest.

What NOT to do

Three hard rules: (1) Never paste client notes or session content into free ChatGPT, Claude, Notion AI, or any consumer AI tool. They train on your inputs — that would be a HIPAA violation. Always use HIPAA-compliant tools with a signed BAA. (2) Always get explicit client consent before AI listens to or transcribes a session. Add it to your intake paperwork. (3) Never let AI make clinical decisions — it's a documentation tool, not a treatment planner. You review, edit, and sign everything.
