AI Tools for Physiotherapists in 2026

Physiotherapy is hands-on work that resists automation — but the paperwork, exercise prescription, and adherence tracking around it absolutely don't. Here's the AI stack that's quietly transforming physio practice in 2026.

📅 July 21, 2026 · 9 min read · By ToolForge Editorial

Physios spend roughly 40% of their day on tasks that aren't treating patients: writing notes, building exercise programs, chasing no-shows, and re-explaining the same home program to patients who lost the handout. The 2026 AI stack attacks each of those specifically — without touching the manual therapy that is the actual job.

The pattern across every clinic we surveyed: the physios who adopted AI tools didn't see fewer patients. They saw more, because each one took less administrative overhead — and the patients got better adherence because the AI handled the boring parts of compliance.

1. Exercise prescription — PhysiApp / MedBridge AI

The classic physio problem: you draw a stick-figure exercise on the back of a handout, the patient loses it, and adherence collapses. PhysiApp and MedBridge have video libraries of thousands of exercises that you assemble into a program in under two minutes. In 2026, both added AI that suggests progressions, flags contraindications (e.g. loading a freshly repaired rotator cuff too aggressively), and auto-adjusts sets/reps as the patient reports in.

Cost: PhysiApp free for patients, clinician plans ~$15-30/mo; MedBridge ~$200-400/yr per clinician.

2. Movement analysis — Kinematic / Physitrack AI Vision

This is the genuinely new category. Patients film themselves doing their home program on a phone; the AI compares joint angles and movement quality against the prescribed form and flags deviations. For remote/virtual physio especially, this catches the "they were doing it wrong for three weeks" problem before it costs you a relapse. Physitrack's AI Vision and the newer Kinematic app lead the field.

Cost: Physitrack ~$30-50/mo per clinician; Kinematic usage-based.

3. Clinical documentation — Nabla / Heidi

Same story as in midwifery: ambient AI scribes that listen to the assessment, draft the SOAP note, and let you sign off. Nabla and Heidi are both HIPAA-eligible with BAAs. Physios report the notes are actually better than their hurried handwritten ones — the AI captures the patient's own words about pain and mechanism of injury, which often gets lost in translation. Saves 60-90 minutes of charting per day.

Cost: Nabla ~$130-180/provider/mo; Heidi ~$80-120/provider/mo.

4. Adherence & follow-up — Spruce / Luma Health

The single biggest predictor of physio outcome is whether the patient did the exercises. Automated SMS check-ins ("Did you do your 3x10 reps today? Reply Y/N") double adherence in study after study. Luma Health and Spruce both do this with AI that escalates non-responders to you, so you only call the patients who actually need a nudge.

Cost: Luma Health ~$150-300/mo per location; Spruce ~$50/provider/mo.

5. Patient education content — ChatGPT (with templates)

"What's a meniscus tear and do I really need to avoid stairs?" — every physio answers this 50 times a week. A custom GPT with your practice's patient-friendly explanations, written at a 7th-grade reading level and templated for the top 30 conditions you see, drafts a personalized handout in seconds. No PHI in the prompt — keep it generic and condition-level only.

Cost: ChatGPT Plus $20/mo.

The stack at a glance

A solo physio can run a credible stack for $50-100/mo (exercise app + ChatGPT + adherence SMS). A full clinic adds the ambient scribe at $130-180/provider/mo — and that line item pays for itself within a week on recovered charting time alone. The movement-analysis add-on is the highest-upside new tool for any practice doing virtual or hybrid care.

What AI can't do for physios

It can't feel a capsular pattern with its hands, sense the quality of a tissue release, or read the patient who's guarding because the last clinician pushed too hard. Manual assessment is a hands-on skill that depends on proprioception AI doesn't have. The stack above doesn't replace the therapist at the plinth — it clears the desk so they can be fully present there.
