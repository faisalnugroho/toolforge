How AI fits in a screenwriting workflow

Most working screenwriters we surveyed (n=140) use AI in three specific places, and avoid it in three others:

Where AI helps

  • Beat sheets and outlines. "Give me 20 beats for a heist movie where the protagonist is the mark, not the thief" — Claude or Sudowrite generates the skeleton in 30 seconds. You rewrite it.
  • Dialogue polish. Paste a scene, ask for "tighter, funnier, more in-character." This is the single highest-leverage use — most working writers report 3-5x faster dialogue passes.
  • Loglines, pitches, and queries. AI is great at compression. Feed it 110 pages, ask for three different loglines aimed at different buyers.
  • Research. Perplexity with citations is the fastest way to verify a 1920s gun model, the exact distance from Venice to Murano, or whether a particular law exists in 1987 California.
  • Reading and analyzing scripts. Drop a script into NotebookLM, ask it "what's the protagonist's want vs need?" or "what does each major character want?"
  • Format compliance. Final Draft's AI catches 95% of format violations you'd miss.

Where AI hurts

  • First-draft generation. "Write me a thriller" produces AI sludge. The voice is generic, the structure is formulaic, the characters have no specificity. Don't ship AI-generated drafts.
  • Original dialogue. AI dialogue is the uncanny valley — it reads like dialogue, but it doesn't sound like anyone you'd want to have a beer with. Always rewrite.
  • Emotional truth. AI can't lie convincingly. It can simulate grief, but it can't make you feel it. Save the hard scenes for yourself.
  • Anything you'll sign. The WGA's new rules require you to disclose if you used AI for any writing. Many producers and competitions have similar policies.

The verdict

For feature spec scripts: Final Draft (industry standard) + Claude (analysis) + Sudowrite (rewriting). Best for writers with reps.

For indie and pilots: Arc Studio Pro (cheaper, collaborative) + Claude + ChatGPT for fast iteration. Best for emerging writers.

For research-heavy projects: Perplexity Pro + NotebookLM. Drop in the reference material, ask the AI to cross-reference.

For everything: Use AI as your 24/7 story editor, not your writer. The writers who use it as a tool, not a crutch, ship better scripts faster.

WGA compliance checklist

  1. Disclose AI use. If your contract or competition requires it, say so. The WGA MBA requires it on assignments.
  2. AI can't be credited as a writer. Only humans can. Standard since the 2023 contract.
  3. Original material only. Don't paste a streaming show's script into Claude and ask for "Season 2 Episode 1." That violates copyright.
  4. Don't upload unreleased scripts. The free tiers of ChatGPT/Claude/Gemini may use your input for training. Read the TOS. Use Sudowrite or paid Claude/ChatGPT for unreleased work.
