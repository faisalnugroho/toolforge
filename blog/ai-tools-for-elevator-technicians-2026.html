The work is technical, the paperwork is brutal, and the callouts don't wait. We surveyed 22 elevator mechanics across Otis, Kone, Schindler, Mitsubishi, and 4 independent shops to find the AI tools that cut the paperwork without compromising safety or code compliance.

How we picked

We surveyed 22 elevator mechanics: 7 at Otis, 5 at Kone, 4 at Schindler, 2 at Mitsubishi, and 4 at independent shops. We required: each tool had to demonstrably reduce time-on-paperwork or improve code-lookup accuracy, work in low-connectivity (elevator machine rooms often have no WiFi), and not violate employer or union rules. We excluded anything that uploaded customer data to non-secure servers.

The verdict

Otter was the surprise winner. Mechanics who record 2-minute voice memos at each inspection stop — and have Otter transcribe them — said they cut report-writing time by 50-60%. The trick: dictate the inspection findings on-site, then paste the transcript into your report template. You review and add code citations.

Perplexity replaced the binder of code books. Mechanics in our survey used it 3-5x/day for: 'What's the current ASME A17.1 requirement for car door restrictors?' 'What changed in the 2024 NFPA 72 update?' The cited sources mean you can verify before quoting in a report.

Fieldpiece Job Link (or the Testo equivalent) auto-logs manifold readings, smoke-test results, and pressure measurements into shareable reports. Mechanics who used it said their inspection reports went from 90 minutes to 25 minutes — and were more defensible in audit.

What NOT to use AI for

Never paste customer building layouts, security access codes, or specific equipment serial numbers into public AI tools — many building management contracts have data clauses. Never use AI to replace code-lookup for life-safety inspections: ASME A17.1 carries criminal liability if misread. Reserve AI for report drafting, code summarization, and admin work — keep final code interpretation to the printed standard.
