How chambers are actually using these tools

Casetext + NotebookLM combo is the most common setup. Casetext handles initial case-law research with proper citation chains. NotebookLM then holds the corpus of filings, transcripts, and research, and you query it without risk of hallucinated precedent.

Otter.ai is now standard in many courtrooms for real-time transcripts. Speaker labels are accurate enough for chambers notes; you'll still want a certified court reporter for the official record.

Claude wins for opinion drafting — its 1M-token context can hold an entire case file plus all cited precedent, and its prose sounds more judicial than ChatGPT's (less marketing-speak, fewer em-dashes).

What judges should NOT do with AI

Per the 2024-2025 judicial AI guidance: do not use AI for fact-finding from the record, do not use AI for evidence weighing, do not enter case-specific facts into public AI tools (confidentiality), always verify citations against primary sources, and disclose AI use when required by local rule.

Verdict

The seven tools above represent the safe, sanctioned surface of judicial AI in 2026. Start with NotebookLM (free) for source-grounded document review and Otter.ai for hearing transcripts. Add Casetext when you have a research-heavy docket. Use Claude (in private mode) for opinion drafting. Skip anything that promises "AI judge" or "AI verdict prediction" — those tools are not appropriate for the bench.
