AI Tools for Solar Installers in 2026

Solar is booming, and the bottleneck isn't panels — it's the hours between a lead and a signed contract. AI is now eating the site survey, the system design, the quote, and the permit set. Here's the stack actually moving the needle in 2026.

📅 July 21, 2026 · 9 min read · By ToolForge Editorial

The math for solar in 2026: panels are cheap, labor is scarce, and the contractor that closes a lead fastest wins the job. The AI tools below compress the lead-to-design-to-quote cycle from days to hours — and the crews that adopt them are closing 2-3× more deals per salesperson.

The pattern is consistent across the installers we talked to: AI doesn't replace the roofer or the electrician. It eliminates the desk time between the roof walk and the signed contract — the part of the business where deals go cold.

1. Remote site survey — Aurora Solar / EagleView

The traditional site survey costs a half-day of driving and a roof climb. Aurora Solar uses satellite + LIDAR imagery plus AI to model the roof, detect obstructions (vents, chimneys, skylights), and produce a viable system layout without anyone leaving the office. EagleView adds high-resolution aerial measurement. The result: 80%+ of surveys never need a site visit, and the ones that do are pre-scoped.

Cost: Aurora ~$150-500/mo per user depending on module count; EagleView per-report pricing ~$50-100.

2. System design & shading — Aurora Solar AI / OpenSolar

Once the roof is modeled, AI proposes the panel layout, strings the system, runs the shading analysis hour-by-hour across the year, and produces the production estimate that goes on the quote. OpenSolar is the strong free-tier challenger to Aurora — both now generate a compliant one-line diagram automatically. What used to take a designer a day now takes minutes.

Cost: OpenSolar free / pay-per-project; Aurora included in subscription.

3. Quoting & financing — Sunbase / OpenSolar Proposals

The quote is where deals die — a 48-hour delay kills conversion. Modern AI quoting engines pull the utility rate tariff for the customer's ZIP, model the financing options (cash, loan, lease, PPA), and produce a branded proposal with lifetime savings, payback period, and ROI in under five minutes. The customer signs on a tablet in the kitchen instead of waiting for a follow-up email.

Cost: Bundled into Aurora/OpenSolar; standalone tools $50-200/mo.

4. Permitting — Sunnumber / Scanifly permits

AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction) permitting is the unglamorous bottleneck that stalls every install. AI-assisted permitting tools pre-fill the AHJ-specific forms, auto-generate the structural and electrical one-line drawings, and flag the redline items that get plans rejected. The good ones cut permit turnaround from 2-4 weeks to 3-7 days.

Cost: ~$25-75 per permit set; some bundles include it.

5. Crew dispatch & project management — Jobber / ServiceTitan AI

Once a job is sold, the logistics begin — materials ordering, crew scheduling, inspection booking. Jobber (smaller shops) and ServiceTitan (larger) both added AI that auto-dispatches the nearest qualified crew, optimizes the route across multiple installs, and texts the homeowner a 30-minute arrival window. Jobber's AI even drafts the post-install follow-up email asking for a review.

Cost: Jobber ~$69-200/mo; ServiceTitan enterprise pricing.

6. Lead intake & CRM — HighLevel / HubSpot AI

Incoming leads from door-knockers, web forms, and referrals need instant response — the first contractor to reply usually wins. HighLevel's AI replies to leads in under 60 seconds, qualifies them, and books the site survey automatically. HubSpot's AI does similar work for installers running a more traditional sales pipeline. Either way, the AI never sleeps and never forgets to follow up.

Cost: HighLevel ~$97-297/mo; HubSpot ~$20-90/user/mo.

The stack at a glance

A small solar shop can run the full stack for $300-500/mo and recover it on the first deal it wins that would otherwise have gone cold. A 5-crew installer typically pays for everything on a single extra closed job per month — and the good stacks close many more than one extra.

What AI can't do for solar installers

It can't carry a panel up a ladder, torque a lug nut to spec, or talk a nervous homeowner through the financing decision at their kitchen table. The physical and relational parts of solar — the actual install and the actual sale — remain firmly human. The stack above exists to compress everything that happens around those two moments so installers spend more time doing the work only they can do.
