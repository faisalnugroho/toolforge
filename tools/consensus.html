Google Scholar, but with answers

Consensus is a search engine that only indexes peer-reviewed scientific papers. Ask 'Does creatine improve memory?' and it synthesizes an answer from 50+ studies, showing the consensus (yes, in specific populations) and citing every paper. The Pro tier adds GPT-4 synthesis, study-level detail, and the 'Consensus Meter' that visualizes the strength of scientific agreement on any claim. Used by academics, journalists, and evidence-based professionals.

Who it's for: Researchers, academics, journalists, medical professionals, and anyone who needs scientifically-backed answers. Especially valuable for health, nutrition, and psychology questions where misinformation is rampant.

Key features

🔬 Peer-Reviewed Only

Index is restricted to papers from journals with editorial review. No blog posts, no Medium articles, no SEO content farms. Just science.

📊 Consensus Meter

Visual representation of how strongly the literature agrees. 'Strong consensus for,' 'mixed evidence,' 'no consensus.' Instant at-a-glance credibility check.

🧠 GPT-4 Synthesis (Pro)

Pro tier synthesizes findings across studies into readable answers with citations. The 'TLDR for 50 papers' feature.

📈 Study Quality Filters

Filter by sample size, study type (RCT, meta-analysis, observational), and population. Critical for evaluating evidence strength.

The honest take

✓ What works

  • Only peer-reviewed sources — no more 'wellness guru' misinformation
  • Consensus Meter is unique and immediately useful
  • Pro tier is cheap ($9/mo) for the value provided
  • Perfect for health/nutrition/psychology questions where evidence matters
  • Excellent for journalists, researchers, and skeptical professionals

✗ What doesn't

  • Limited to science — useless for general knowledge questions
  • Free tier is too restrictive (20 searches/mo) for regular use
  • Some legitimate questions have no peer-reviewed research yet
  • Studies can be contradictory; Consensus surfaces this but doesn't resolve it

Verdict

If you do any evidence-based work — research, medicine, journalism, policy — Consensus is essential. It's not a Perplexity replacement, it's a complement. Use Perplexity for general research, Consensus for science-grounded answers.
