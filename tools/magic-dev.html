Long-horizon coding, not just tab completion

Magic.dev (Magic.dev, Inc.) is one of the more ambitious bets in AI coding: build a frontier model whose entire purpose is to reason over long sessions with an engineer — across many files, many turns, and many hours. Their 2024 LTM-2-mini demo showed 100M-token context. In 2025–2026 they shipped research previews aimed at engineering teams who feel Copilot-class autocomplete is hitting a ceiling.

Who it's for: Senior engineers and teams working in large, unfamiliar codebases where a 200K-token window isn't enough — think platform migrations, multi-repo refactors, and deep debugging sessions that span hours.

Key features

LTMLong-context memory

Engineered for million-token-plus context windows with retrieval tuned for code rather than prose. Useful for "read the whole monorepo, then answer" workflows.

PairMulti-hour sessions

Designed to keep state across many turns — you can reference a decision made 90 minutes ago without re-pasting context. Closer to a junior engineer's working memory than a chatbot.

CodeTrained for engineering

Built specifically for code understanding and generation rather than a general-purpose LLM fine-tuned for code. Their pitch: tooling matters less when the base model is right.

Pricing

Free during research preview (waitlisted). No public paid tier pricing as of July 2026 — Magic.dev is targeting enterprise and team contracts rather than individual subscriptions. Expect this to be priced like an engineering seat, not like a $20/mo chatbot.

Pros & cons

Pros
  • Genuinely long context — useful for monorepos
  • Built for engineers, not for churning marketing copy
  • Free during preview
  • Backed by serious capital ($465M raised)
Cons
  • Waitlisted — not generally available
  • No IDE plugin yet (vs Cursor, Copilot)
  • Pricing likely enterprise-only when GA
  • Young — fewer benchmarks than frontier rivals
